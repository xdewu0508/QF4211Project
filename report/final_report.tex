\section{Introduction}

Cryptocurrency markets trade continuously and lack conventional valuation anchors such as cash flows or earnings. As a result, market participants rely heavily on price action, volume, and technical indicators, while machine-learning models promise greater flexibility in capturing non-linear and sequential structure. The practical issue, however, is not whether a model can generate directional predictions in isolation, but whether those signals survive a realistic backtest once transaction costs and portfolio turnover are taken seriously.

This study therefore asks the following question: To what extent do ML-based trading strategies outperform traditional rule-based strategies and passive buy-and-hold benchmarks in terms of the Sharpe Ratio, once transaction costs and backtesting constraints are applied to major assets such as Bitcoin (BTC), Ethereum (ETH), Ripple (XRP), and Solana (SOL)? We evaluate four machine-learning models (Logistic Regression, XGBoost, LightGBM, and GRU), two traditional rule-based strategies (SMA and RSI), and a buy-and-hold benchmark under long-only, short-only, and long-short structures.

Our contribution is a cost-aware, cross-asset comparison with an additional fixed-window robustness check rather than a claim that machine learning consistently dominates cryptocurrency trading. The main analysis uses the canonical 2021--2025 sample, and the report later compares it with a 2020--2024 rerun that keeps the same pipeline, feature construction, split ratios, models, thresholding rules, and backtesting assumptions. Across both windows, long-only strategies are the most stable active structure and machine-learning models provide pockets of improvement in selected settings, yet they do not deliver robust, statistically significant outperformance over simpler active rules or passive buy-and-hold within the current evaluation design.

\section{Literature Review}

Because cryptocurrency assets do not generate cash flows or dividends, technical indicators remain a common decision framework. Hudson and Urquhart (2021) show that technical rules can outperform passive exposure in selected cryptocurrency settings, but Liu et al. (2024) note that indicators such as RSI do not always behave in crypto the way they do in traditional asset classes. Transaction-cost assumptions are also pivotal: Svogun and Baz\'an-Palomino (2022) show that fees can materially change profitability assessments, while studies such as Ratia (2023) and Anghel (2021) focus mainly on long-only designs. The literature therefore leaves open how well rule-based trading survives realistic costs across richer position structures.

Against that backdrop, machine-learning approaches have become increasingly common in cryptocurrency forecasting, with linear classifiers, boosted trees, and recurrent networks all used to capture non-linear and temporal structure. Chatzis et al. (2025) report strong results for models such as XGBoost and recurrent networks, but those gains are often studied in more favorable settings than a fee-aware backtest. Belalova et al. (2023) argue that GRU can outperform LSTM when data are limited because its simpler gating structure reduces overfitting risk, while Jabbar and Jalil (2024) emphasize that apparent predictive gains often weaken sharply out of sample. That tension between modelling flexibility and trading robustness motivates our model set.

Against this literature, our project sits between replication and extension and is anchored mainly in Goutte et al. (2023), \textit{Deep learning and technical analysis in cryptocurrency market}. We retain its hourly, feature-based framing, but compare ML models, rule-based strategies, and buy-and-hold across four assets and three strategy structures under a common \texttt{0.035\%} transaction fee per unit position change. The study prioritizes economic evaluation rather than raw classification quality, and the report also adds a shifted 2020--2024 rerun to test sensitivity to the evaluation window while keeping the substantive claims deliberately scoped to the submitted design.

\section{Data Description and Preprocessing}

\subsection{Data Source and Assets}

The dataset is drawn from Binance public hourly candlestick files for four liquid USDT spot pairs: BTC/USDT, ETH/USDT, XRP/USDT, and SOL/USDT. These assets were selected to provide a mix of large-cap and higher-beta behavior while keeping the market venue, fee assumptions, and data structure consistent across the analysis. BTC and ETH represent larger-cap assets with deeper institutional participation, while XRP and SOL add higher-beta and more regime-sensitive behavior.

\subsection{Sample Period}

The canonical raw hourly files span January 2021 to December 2025. After forward-return alignment, the main notebook works with \texttt{43,808} usable hourly observations per asset. This window covers the 2021 bull market, the 2022 drawdown, the 2023 recovery, the 2024 ETF-driven institutional surge, and the volatile conditions late in 2025. To test whether the later sample materially changes the conclusions, we also rerun the same workflow on a shifted 2020--2024 window in a sandbox environment and compare the results in Section~5 and Appendix~\ref{app:regime}. That rerun strengthens the regime-sensitivity discussion, but it remains a fixed-window comparison rather than full walk-forward validation.

Hourly data were retained to remain aligned with the reference paper and to preserve an intraday decision horizon, while still being coarse enough to avoid the extreme noise and execution burden of sub-hourly strategies. Even so, hourly signals remain noisy and turnover-sensitive, which is important when interpreting the final results.

\subsection{Data Construction}

We construct four feature sets. \texttt{ohlc\_raw} uses open, high, low, close, volume, trade count, and cyclical hour terms. \texttt{candle\_raw} replaces the raw OHLC block with candlestick body and shadow representations while retaining volume, trade count, and cyclical terms. \texttt{ohlc\_extended} and \texttt{candle\_extended} augment the corresponding raw sets with technical indicators including SMA, RSI, MACD line and histogram, Williams \%R, stochastic oscillator, and MFI. All features are lagged so that only information available at or before the current hour is used when generating a trade for the next-hour open entry and the following-hour open exit.

\subsection{Train / Validation / Test Split}

Each asset is divided chronologically into \texttt{50\%} training, \texttt{25\%} validation, and \texttt{25\%} testing. Under the main 2021--2025 notebook logic this yields \texttt{21,902} training rows, \texttt{10,950} validation rows, and \texttt{10,952} test rows per asset after forward-return alignment and split-boundary trimming. The same protocol is reapplied to the shifted 2020--2024 rerun so that the robustness comparison changes the window, not the design. The evidence should therefore be read as two fixed-window out-of-sample evaluations, not as proof that the same rankings would persist under walk-forward re-estimation.

\section{Methodology and Model Explanation}

\subsection{Prediction Labelling}

The predictive task is framed as two binary classification problems built on the next-hour open-to-open log return. The strategy observes the current feature vector, enters at the next hour’s open if a threshold is triggered, and exits one hour later at the following open. We define:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{label\_up = 1} when the next-hour open-to-open log return is positive,
    \item \texttt{label\_down = 1} when the next-hour open-to-open log return is negative.
\end{itemize}

This dual-labelling design allows long-only, short-only, and long-short trading structures to be implemented consistently across both machine-learning and rule-based strategies.

Class imbalance is limited in the submitted chronological split. Across the four training sets, the share of \texttt{label\_up = 1} ranges from \texttt{49.2\%} to \texttt{50.6\%}, while the share of \texttt{label\_down = 1} ranges from \texttt{48.3\%} to \texttt{49.6\%}; the residual corresponds to zero-return hours. Severe one-sided label dominance is therefore not the main driver of the reported results.

\subsection{Strategy Families}

\subsubsection{Buy-and-Hold Benchmark}

Buy-and-hold maintains a constant long position over the full test period and serves as the passive benchmark. In the \texttt{with\_cost} setting, the benchmark incurs the one-time initial entry cost implied by moving from zero exposure to a fully invested long position.

\subsubsection{Rule-Based Technical Heuristics}

SMA and RSI serve as rule-based benchmarks. For SMA, short windows of \texttt{5}, \texttt{10}, \texttt{20}, and \texttt{30} hours are paired with long windows of \texttt{50}, \texttt{100}, \texttt{150}, and \texttt{200} hours. For RSI, windows of \texttt{7}, \texttt{14}, and \texttt{21} hours are tested. Each rule produces a continuous score, which is then mapped through the same validation and thresholding pipeline used for the machine-learning strategies. The long-only, short-only, and long-short structures therefore apply to SMA and RSI as well, rather than only to the ML models.

\subsubsection{Machine-Learning Models}

Four machine-learning models are evaluated. Logistic Regression provides a linear probability baseline. XGBoost and LightGBM represent tree-based ensembles that capture non-linear feature interactions. GRU is the sequential model, using a 24-hour lookback window to learn temporal dependencies in the input features. The GRU architecture used in the notebook is a compact recurrent model with a 16-unit GRU layer followed by a dense layer, dropout, and a sigmoid output.

\subsection{Backtesting Framework}

Backtests are executed under both \texttt{no\_cost} and \texttt{with\_cost} regimes. The \texttt{with\_cost} setting applies a transaction cost of \texttt{0.035\%} per unit position change. Opening or closing a unit position therefore costs \texttt{0.035\%}, while directly flipping from long to short costs \texttt{0.07\%}. This design penalizes high-turnover strategies and makes it possible to separate raw predictive opportunity from economic viability after friction.

All portfolios are evaluated from a normalized initial capital of \texttt{1}. Positions are unit-scaled (\texttt{+1} long, \texttt{0} flat, \texttt{-1} short), so capital utilisation changes through time-in-market rather than through leverage sizing; this is why turnover and trading frequency are reported alongside the return-based metrics.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics and Feasibility Rules}

Following the reference paper, the main benchmark is built from compounded signal returns rather than annualized hourly returns. Using the paper's notation,
\[
\text{Total Return} = \prod_{i=1}^{N}(1 + R_i S_i)
\]
\[
\text{Average Return} = \frac{\text{Total Return}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} S_i}
\]
\[
\text{Sharpe Ratio} = \frac{\text{Average Return}}{\text{Standard Deviation of Return}}
\]
where \(R_i\) is the realized one-period return and \(S_i \in \{0,1\}\) indicates whether a signal is active at time \(i\). In the notebook, this is implemented on the realized active net-return sequence \(r_j\) by using \((\prod_{j=1}^{N_{\text{sig}}}(1+r_j)-1)/N_{\text{sig}}\) in the numerator and dividing by the standard deviation of the signal-return sequence. This remains the headline Sharpe metric in the summary tables.

We also report annualized Sharpe as a secondary diagnostic:
\[
\text{Annualized Sharpe} =
\frac{\mu(r_t - r_f / 8760)}{\sigma(r_t - r_f / 8760)} \sqrt{8760}
\]
where \(r_t\) denotes hourly net returns and \(r_f = 0\) in the submitted implementation.

Thresholds are selected on the validation set using a precision-first rule subject to feasibility constraints. A candidate threshold must generate at least \texttt{20} trades, a signal ratio of at least \texttt{0.2\%}, and recall of at least \texttt{1\%} before it is considered feasible. Among feasible thresholds, precision is prioritized, with recall and validation Sharpe used only as secondary tie-breakers. The report’s main tables, grouped statistical tests, and representative graphs focus on these feasible configurations, while infeasible cases are retained separately in diagnostic exports.

\section{Model Performance Evaluation and Analysis}

\subsection{Overall Performance Comparison}

Appendix~\ref{app:tables} contains the supporting tables for the canonical 2021--2025 run, while Appendix~\ref{app:regime} contains the window-comparison tables and figures for the additional 2020--2024 rerun. Table~\ref{tab:appendix_overall_performance} shows the main-window ranking clearly: long-only is the strongest active structure, with average feasible test Sharpe \texttt{0.0495} in the no-cost case and \texttt{0.0212} after costs, while long-short falls to \texttt{0.0006} after costs and short-only remains negative. Table~\ref{tab:appendix_c_strategy_mode} preserves the same ranking but at a higher performance level in the earlier window: long-only rises to \texttt{0.0767} in the no-cost case and \texttt{0.0484} with costs, and long-short improves to \texttt{0.0433} and \texttt{0.0171}. Short-only does not share that improvement, moving from \texttt{0.0005} to \texttt{-0.0036} in the no-cost case and remaining near \texttt{-0.024} after costs. The ranking is therefore stable, but the level of performance is clearly regime-sensitive.

Buy-and-hold also remains a difficult benchmark in both windows. Table~\ref{tab:appendix_buyhold} shows that in the canonical test set the benchmark annualized Sharpe ratios are \texttt{0.82} for BTC, \texttt{0.53} for ETH, \texttt{1.40} for XRP, and \texttt{0.25} for SOL. Table~\ref{tab:appendix_c_buyhold} then shows that passive exposure is itself much stronger in the earlier window: BTC cumulative return rises from \texttt{0.4010} to \texttt{2.2999}, ETH from \texttt{0.1673} to \texttt{0.9302}, XRP from \texttt{2.0132} to \texttt{2.9675}, and SOL from \texttt{-0.1723} to \texttt{2.3422}. The earlier window is therefore friendlier not only to active strategies but also to the passive benchmark, which is why better absolute ML performance does not automatically imply better relative performance.

\subsection{Impact of Transaction Costs}

The \texttt{no\_cost} versus \texttt{with\_cost} comparison shows that transaction costs are not a secondary detail; they are central to the ranking of strategies in both windows. Table~\ref{tab:appendix_overall_performance} shows that in 2021--2025, long-only average cumulative return falls from \texttt{0.2397} to \texttt{-0.1065} when costs are introduced. Appendix~\ref{app:regime} shows the same mechanism in 2020--2024: long-only falls from \texttt{0.4604} to \texttt{0.0292}, and long-short falls from \texttt{0.3851} to \texttt{-0.2100}. The earlier regime is more forgiving, but it does not remove the economic burden of repeated position changes.

The same point appears in the positive-Sharpe shares reported in Table~\ref{tab:appendix_c_strategy_mode}. In the with-cost setting, the share of positive-Sharpe feasible configurations rises from \texttt{50.0\%} to \texttt{73.6\%} for long-only and from \texttt{26.4\%} to \texttt{47.2\%} for long-short when the window is moved earlier, yet short-only falls from \texttt{19.4\%} to \texttt{13.9\%}. More complex strategies can generate more signals, but signal volume is not automatically a virtue when each position change incurs a cost, and a friendlier regime does not rescue every structure equally.

\subsection{Model-Level Comparison}

At the family level, Table~\ref{tab:appendix_model_family} and Table~\ref{tab:appendix_c_model_family} show that ML strategies are somewhat stronger than the rule-based benchmarks, but the advantage is regime-dependent. In the canonical 2021--2025 window, the average feasible test Sharpe is \texttt{0.0275} for ML models versus \texttt{-0.0012} for rule-based strategies in the no-cost case, and \texttt{-0.0004} versus \texttt{-0.0056} after costs. In the 2020--2024 rerun, the corresponding ML averages improve to \texttt{0.0435} and \texttt{0.0162}, while the rule-based averages move only to \texttt{0.0011} and \texttt{-0.0050}. ML therefore benefits more from the earlier, more favorable window, especially after costs, but the advantage remains modest rather than decisive.

Within the ML group, the outputs show a split picture rather than a single dominant winner. Tables~\ref{tab:appendix_table12_nocost} and \ref{tab:appendix_table12_withcost} show that the canonical all-asset long-only averages are led by GRU in both cost regimes, with Logistic Regression also strong and XGBoost weakest on average. The earlier window broadens the leadership set instead of confirming one universal winner: Table~\ref{tab:appendix_c_best_models} shows GRU, LightGBM, and Logistic Regression splitting the best-overall slots evenly in 2020--2024, whereas the 2021--2025 run is dominated by GRU and Logistic Regression. Extra model complexity therefore does not guarantee more robust trading performance.

\subsection{Feature Representation and Strategy Behaviour}

Within the feasible ML configurations, Tables~\ref{tab:appendix_table10_withcost} and \ref{tab:appendix_table11_withcost} show the main-window representation result clearly: standard \texttt{ohlc} inputs outperform \texttt{candle} inputs in the grouped all-asset long-only with-cost comparison (test Sharpe \texttt{0.0255} versus \texttt{0.0195}), while extended feature sets outperform raw feature sets much more clearly (\texttt{0.0472} versus \texttt{-0.0024}). The current notebook therefore does not support the idea that candlestick inputs are broadly superior, but it does support the view that the paper-aligned extended indicators can help in selected settings. Table~\ref{tab:appendix_c_significance} reinforces that conclusion in the earlier window: significant grouped bootstrap results increase from \texttt{11/90} to \texttt{23/90}, and several of the additional significant rows are \texttt{extended\_vs\_raw} wins. Table~\ref{tab:appendix_c_best_features} similarly shifts the best-overall feature counts toward \texttt{candle\_extended} and \texttt{ohlc\_extended} only. Predictive improvements therefore become easier to detect in the earlier window, yet they remain economically conditional because richer features can still coincide with higher trading activity.

The behavior of short-only strategies deserves explicit discussion because the underperformance is one of the most stable findings in the project. In the canonical with-cost setting, short-only strategies have a negative average Sharpe for every asset, ranging from \texttt{-0.0146} in BTC to \texttt{-0.0345} in ETH. Table~\ref{tab:appendix_c_strategy_mode} shows that the earlier window does not change that diagnosis: the average no-cost short-only Sharpe slips from \texttt{0.0005} to \texttt{-0.0036}, and the with-cost average remains around \texttt{-0.024}. This appears to reflect two forces: crypto markets retain enough upside drift that a pure short stance is structurally disadvantaged, and short-only strategies still trade frequently---about \texttt{1,564} trades on average in the later window and \texttt{1,382} in the earlier one---so even modest predictive errors are quickly magnified by repeated transaction costs.

\subsection{Statistical Significance}

The notebook’s main statistical layer uses grouped block-bootstrap tests on feasible ML configurations rather than exhaustive pairwise model-versus-model comparisons. These grouped tests compare \texttt{candle} versus \texttt{ohlc} inputs, \texttt{extended} versus \texttt{raw} feature sets, and \texttt{GRU} versus the rest of the ML set across asset scopes, strategy modes, and cost regimes. Table~\ref{tab:appendix_sigtests} shows that in the canonical 2021--2025 run, statistical significance is concentrated in a narrow subset of tests rather than appearing broadly across the grid. At the all-asset level, extended features outperform raw features in the with-cost short-only and long-short comparisons, and the \texttt{GRU}-versus-rest contrast is significant in those same with-cost grouped tests.

Table~\ref{tab:appendix_c_significance} shows that the earlier window yields more statistically detectable structure, but not a different bottom line. Significant grouped bootstrap results increase from \texttt{11/90} to \texttt{23/90}, and significant positive-usefulness tests versus zero rise from \texttt{4/24} to \texttt{9/24}. That means the 2020--2024 rerun makes more configurations look viable in absolute terms, especially for long-only and extended-feature ML setups. However, the same table also shows that the number of best-overall configurations that significantly outperform buy-and-hold remains \texttt{0} in both windows. Even where some configurations show positive Sharpe ratios or cumulative returns, the evidence of persistent alpha therefore remains narrow and unstable.

\subsection{Equity Curve Analysis}

The equity-curve figures are presented in Appendix~\ref{app:figures}, and the regime-comparison figures are collected in Appendix~\ref{app:regime}. The main-window equity curves show the asset-by-asset shape of the canonical 2021--2025 backtests, while the Appendix C figures summarize the cross-window comparison in aggregate.
The long-only curves in Appendix~\ref{app:fig_long_only} show the clearest active-trading potential: BTC and XRP remain dominated by buy-and-hold after costs, ETH narrows the gap in selected configurations, and SOL is more competitive but also more uneven. The long-short curves in Appendix~\ref{app:fig_long_short} are noisier in all four assets and visually reinforce the cost drag already shown in the tables. The short-only curves in Appendix~\ref{app:fig_short_only} are the clearest confirmation of persistent weakness, with several best-per-model paths decaying steadily after costs. The Appendix C figures show the same story in aggregate: Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_c_strategy_mode} captures the strategy-mode shift, Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_c_model_family} the model-family shift, and Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_c_buyhold} the stronger passive benchmark in the earlier window.

\section{Conclusion, Limitations, and Future Work}

\subsection{Conclusion}

Within the canonical 2021--2025 evaluation window, the answer to our research question is: only to a limited extent, and not in any broad or robust sense. Machine-learning models can improve on simple rule-based strategies in selected settings, especially before costs, but they do not consistently or statistically reliably outperform buy-and-hold once transaction costs are applied.

The additional 2020--2024 robustness rerun changes the strength of the results more than their ranking. Long-only and long-short strategies---especially ML configurations---become materially stronger in the earlier window, and more grouped tests and usefulness tests turn significant. That confirms that the framework is regime-sensitive rather than uniformly weak. Even so, the broader conclusion does not flip. Short-only remains weak across both windows, transaction costs continue to erode long-short and high-turnover strategies, and none of the best configurations significantly outperform buy-and-hold in either sample. The evidence therefore supports a narrow claim: ML-based cryptocurrency strategies can outperform simple rules in selected settings, and they look better in friendlier regimes, but they do not deliver broad, cost-robust, statistically reliable superiority over passive buy-and-hold.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations materially qualify the findings. First, even with the additional 2020--2024 rerun, the study still relies on fixed-window comparisons rather than rolling or expanding walk-forward retraining. The second window improves the regime-sensitivity evidence, but it does not show whether the observed rankings would persist under continuously updated historical partitions.

Second, the predictive target is binary directional classification rather than return regression. A correct prediction on a very small move is therefore treated the same as a correct prediction on a much larger economically important move. The thresholding rules partially mitigate this by filtering for higher-confidence signals, but they do not remove the mismatch between directional classification and return magnitude.

Third, the backtest remains stylized. It applies exchange-style transaction fees, but it does not model slippage, execution delay, order-book depth, market impact, borrowing costs for shorting, margin requirements, or liquidation dynamics.

Fourth, the models trade at hourly frequency. This preserves an intraday research design and aligns with the reference paper, but it also increases exposure to noise and to turnover-driven cost drag.

Finally, the dataset is exchange-specific and limited to four assets, so some conclusions may nor reflect the full cryptocurrency universe. The current version also does not include a dedicated explainability analysis or formal feature-selection stage, so it does not isolate which indicators contribute most to predictive performance or test dimensionality-reduction alternatives systematically.

\subsection{Future Work}

The main contribution of the project is comparative and cautionary rather than celebratory. Under two fixed historical windows and a realistic fee model, the practical value of active cryptocurrency trading remains limited, and the margin between machine learning and simpler alternatives is much narrower than headline predictive results might suggest. Future work should move from fixed-window comparison to walk-forward re-estimation, add explicit regime labelling, incorporate richer backtest frictions, explore lower-frequency variants, and extend explainability analysis to determine whether any observed advantage survives under stronger robustness checks.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Single train/test split}: One fixed historical window may not generalise across market regimes. (by 2, 4, 5) \textit{Response:} still a limitation, but now partially mitigated by the additional 2020--2024 fixed-window robustness comparison in Section~5 and Appendix~\ref{app:regime}.
    \item \textbf{Regime-conditioned analysis}: Reporting results by regime would strengthen the generalisation claim. (by 2, 3) \textit{Response:} partially addressed via the additional 2020--2024 robustness rerun in Section~5 and Appendix~\ref{app:regime}; formal regime labelling and walk-forward analysis remain future work.
    \item \textbf{Binary classification target}: Direction-only prediction ignores return magnitude. (by 2, 3, 4) \textit{Response:} acknowledged as a limitation in \emph{Limitations}.
    \item \textbf{Sharpe formulation and code consistency}: Sharpe definitions were unclear. (mainly by 2) \textit{Response:} fixed in \emph{Evaluation Metrics and Feasibility Rules}, which now states the paper formula and distinguishes annualized Sharpe.
    \item \textbf{Short-only underperformance unexplained}: The consistent weakness of short-only strategies needed interpretation. (by 1) \textit{Response:} fixed in \emph{Feature Representation and Strategy Behaviour}.
    \item \textbf{Tuning asymmetry}: Reviewers thought ML was tuned while SMA/RSI were not. (by 1) \textit{Response:} rebutted in \emph{Rule-Based Technical Heuristics}; SMA and RSI are validation-tuned too.
    \item \textbf{Transaction-cost sensitivity}: A broader fee sweep was suggested. (by 1, 5) \textit{Response:} we keep the Binance-aligned fee design and treat broader sensitivity analysis as beyond this round.
    \item \textbf{Backtest realism}: Slippage, liquidity, and realistic shorting frictions are omitted. (by 3, 4) \textit{Response:} admitted as a limitation in \emph{Limitations}.
    \item \textbf{Precision-first tuning vs Sharpe objective}: The selection objective looked mismatched with the economic objective. (by 4) \textit{Response:} clarified in \emph{Evaluation Metrics and Feasibility Rules} as a deliberate high-confidence signal design.
    \item \textbf{Noisy hourly frequency}: Hourly trading may be noise-heavy and turnover-heavy. (by 3) \textit{Response:} acknowledged in \emph{Sample Period} and \emph{Limitations}.
    \item \textbf{Cluttered equity curves}: The original bundled plots were too crowded. (by 1) \textit{Response:} fixed by moving the figure set to the appendix and using representative per-model curves.
    \item \textbf{Conclusion did not answer the question directly}: The central conclusion was too indirect. (by 1, 5) \textit{Response:} fixed in \emph{Conclusion}.
    \item \textbf{Feature importance analysis absent}: The report did not explain which indicators drive the extended-feature results. (by 1, 3) \textit{Response:} not added; admitted indirectly via the explainability and feature-selection limitations.
    \item \textbf{Rule-based strategy implementation vague}: It was unclear whether long-only, short-only, and long-short applied to SMA/RSI too. (by 5) \textit{Response:} fixed in \emph{Rule-Based Technical Heuristics}.
    \item \textbf{Infeasible configurations not explained}: Excluded configurations needed a principled explanation. (by 5) \textit{Response:} fixed in \emph{Evaluation Metrics and Feasibility Rules}.

    \item \textbf{Inaccessible GitHub repository}: The original review link was not publicly accessible. (by 1, 2) \textit{Response:} operational issue to be corrected at submission; the repo path is now organized for a public handoff.
    \item \textbf{Redundant notebooks}: Multiple notebooks created ambiguity about the canonical pipeline. (by 1) \textit{Response:} fixed by consolidating the repo around \texttt{project.ipynb}.
    \item \textbf{GRU undocumented}: The most complex model needed clearer documentation. (by 1) \textit{Response:} fixed in \emph{Machine-Learning Models} and the canonical notebook.
