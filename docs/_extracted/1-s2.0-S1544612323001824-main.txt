A B S T R A C T
A large number of modern practices in financial forecasting rely on technical analysis, which
involves several heuristics techniques of price charts visual pattern recognition as well as other
technical indicators. In this study, we aim to investigate the potential use of those technical
information (candlestick information as well as technical indicators) as inputs for machine
learning models, especially the state-of-the-art deep learning algorithms, to generate trading
signals. To properly address this problem, empirical research is conducted which applies several
machine learning methods to 5 years of Bitcoin hourly data from 2017 to 2022. From the result
of our study, we confirm the potential of trading strategies using machine learning approaches.
We also find that among several machine learning models, deep learning models, specifically
the recurrent neural networks, tend to outperform the others in time-series prediction.
1. Introduction
Even since the success of Google’s AlphaGo against the Go World Champion in 2016, the keywords AI (Artificial Intelligence),
machine learning, and deep learning have become the buzzwords that keep being mentioned in almost every industry. As a matter
of fact, machine learning has already been slowly yet steadily transforming your everyday activities.
The finance industry, as expected, is one of the pioneers to incorporate AI technologies into its ecosystems. In recent years,
more and more studies have been published that were based on the application of machine learning models on forecasting time
series and many of them point out the relatively better performances of newer machine learning models compared to classical time
series forecasting techniques (Rodriguez and Rodriguez, 2004; Cosenza et al., 2021; Yun et al., 2021). Additionally, the widespread
and easy-to-access automated electronic trading systems coupled with increasing demand for better alpha keep forcing trading
practitioners and researchers to put more effort into searching for better and more sophisticated models. Not until recently, the
technique of deep learning (DL), a subset of machine learning which involves the application of neural networks, started to emerge
as one of the best-performing techniques within the field of machine learning in a vast majority of implementation areas. It is no
exception in the case of financial time series forecasting as a growing number of deep-learning-based models of prediction have been
introduced in several conferences and journals in recent years. Gu et al. (2020) used neural networks to forecast stock prices from the
US market to find out their superiority over other models such as generalized linear models, principal components regressions, and
regression trees (Gu et al., 2020). Zhang et al. (2021) utilize recurrent neural networks, specifically long short-term memory (LSTM)
networks, to predict stock trends and report significant positive returns ( Zhang et al., 2021). The work of Bianchi et al. (2021), on

2. Dataset
2.1. Data source
Due to the borderless nature of the cryptocurrency market, the prices of cryptocurrencies are not the same everywhere but vary
between exchanges. In this research, we chose the Binance exchange as it is the largest cryptocurrency exchange based on its trading
volume as of August 2022. The Binance exchange publicly offers historical data dating back to 2017. Transaction details are stored
continuously and are accessible via its API 1. The exchange houses several sets of high-frequency data including limit order quotes
and trading logs (or market order logs). Due to the goal of our analysis, only the trading logs (or trade data) are needed. Table 1
lists out all the fields of the trading logs table and their meanings.
The trade data table records every single transaction that had taken place. Each row refers to a transaction processed by the
Binance exchange and obviously, it is possible that many transactions can happen at the same time.
As every transaction is recorded in this table, the total number of transactions provided during our chosen period from the end
of 2017 until August 2022 is up to a few billion. This is an enormous amount of data and is required to be processed for further
analysis.
2.2. Data preprocessing
The first step of data preprocessing is to gather all of the needed data values from different data sources into a single collection
of databases. In this study, as all of our data come from the historical trading database of Binance, there is no need for other sources
of data. However, if any other types of alternative data are considered such as order book data, sentimental or fundamental data,
etc., they should be included in this step. In this study, during these steps, several trivial data such as the ID of a single trade,
type of order, etc. will also be removed. In the end, all of the trades that happened in every hour are resampled and grouped by
their timestamps to create a new table of time series data with the following header: timestamps, open, high, low, close, number of
trades, and volume (see Table 2).
After the resampling procedure, the length of our dataset is reduced to only 43,463 data points. For the purpose of training our
machine learning models, the data will be divided into 3 parts: training set, validation set, and test set with the proportion of 50%,
25%, and 25% respectively based on chronological order as described in Fig. 1.

3. Methodology
3.1. Feature engineering
Candlestick Data
Generally, the asset price data of a specific time period can be represented by four features: the opening price (the price at the
beginning of the period), closing price (price at the end of the period), high price (the maximum price during the period), and low
price (the minimum over the interval). However in technical analysis, the candlestick can be visually encoded in a different way
(Fig. 2): the candle body denotes the opening and closing prices, and the lines protruding from the bar (the candle’s shadow) denote
the extrema over the interval. Finally, the color of the bar implies the relative return of the period: a white (sometimes green) candle
denotes a positive return over the interval (closing price > opening price) and a black (sometimes red) candle denotes a negative
return (closing price < opening price). This alternative representation of the candlestick data may connect closer to how technical
analyst visualize their charts and candlestick pattern and has been used by Ghoshal and Roberts ( 2020).

3.2. Data labeling
For financial time series problems, especially when it relates to a trading system, there is no fixed answer for the data labeling.
If the research wants to predict the future price then it should be a regression problem and the future price of the next data
point should be the label. But for a trading signal generating system, the label can be binary ( [𝐵𝑢𝑦, 𝑁𝑜𝑡 𝐵𝑢𝑦 ]) or multi-class
like [𝐵𝑢𝑦, 𝑆𝑒𝑙𝑙, 𝐻𝑜𝑙𝑑 ] depending on the preference of the researchers as well. In our study, our labels are chosen to be binary
([𝐵𝑢𝑦, 𝑁𝑜𝑡 𝐵𝑢𝑦 ] or [𝑆𝑒𝑙𝑙, 𝑁𝑜𝑡 𝑆𝑒𝑙𝑙 ]) which are similar to trading signals used in technical analysis (specifically in the case of candle
stick patterns where you have bullish patterns and bearish patterns). Furthermore, this choice of two separate systems for long and
short also make it easier the inclusion the prediction with the threshold where we need to search for the best threshold using the
validation set. The dimension for the search will be only one with two separate long and short systems instead of two for systems
that are both long and short. Also with the threshold included, a both long and short system is more similar to a [𝐵𝑢𝑦, 𝑆𝑒𝑙𝑙, 𝐻𝑜𝑙𝑑 ]
system where it does nothing when the prediction is below the threshold to long and above the threshold to short. More details
about this procedure will be explained in a later section. As there is no threshold for the return used, basically we have the signal
for that positive return will be labeled as the signal for buy and vice versa. The ratios of the labels are shown in Table 4.
The prediction horizon is set to the next hour or a candlestick and we set up our trading experiment as follows: For every trading
signal, 1 dollar worth of BTC is either shorted or longed based on the signal. The position will be closed after 1 h unless there is
a subsequent positive signal. The lookback period is also set at 24 h which means that historical data of each feature for the last
24 h will be used as inputs to forecasting the trading decision.

3.4. Model evaluation
Precision and Recall
Precision and recall are also popular metrics that usually go together for evaluating classifier performance and will be used as
the main metrics in this paper.
The increases in either precision or recall are often achieved at the expense of the other, i.e. high precision is achieved at the
expense of recall, and high recall is achieved at the expense of precision. Usually, an ideal model would love to have both high
recall and high precision but this is usually hard to be achieved in practice. Due to the fact that a buy signal does not necessarily
lead to a good return and investors usually look for a good return rather than a normal return, in combination with the existence
of transaction costs, a good trading signal system generally prefers a high precision with a reasonable recall.

Significance Tests
In order to evaluate the relative performance of various model parameters, we not only rely on the previously discussed metrics
but also conduct significance tests to determine if there are any significant differences between groups of models based on different
features or algorithms. To accomplish this, we apply a two-sample t-test to the return of each group of models using the block
bootstrapping method where we divide the data into 𝑛∕𝑘 non-overlapping blocks. 𝑛 is the sample size and 𝑘 is the length of each
block. 𝑘 is chosen to be equal to 𝑛1∕3 as it is the optimal length proposed by Shalizi (2013). Each block is then sampled with
replacement to recreate the original sample sets for 10,000 iterations. As the return samples of each model will have the same size,
all of them will be divided by the same number of blocks with the same timestamps. For each iteration, the same block will be
chosen then across different strategies.
4. Result and discussion
4.1. General discussion
As discussed in the previous section, the goal of our study is not only to find out the potential application of machine learning
in generating trading signals but also to find out whether or not the representation of candlesticks and other popular technical
indicators is necessary for the improvement of the models. Therefore, we applied several subsets of features compared to each other
to compare their usefulness.
The first dimension for the set of features is the representation of the candlestick. In Section 3.1, we have mentioned that the
candlestick has 3 main characteristics: the candle body, the upper shadow, and the lower shadow. In combination with the closing
price, we use them to create the ‘‘candle’’ features dataset to compare with the traditional ‘‘ohlc’’ dataset, which contains open,
high, low, and closing price, to see which approach serve better as the input for the machine learning models.
The second dimension is about the inclusion of additional technical indicators or not. The feature dataset with the inclusion of
additional technical indicators is called the ‘‘extended’’ feature dataset. For the original set of features (only 4 candlestick features,
trades, volume, and the cyclical), we called it the ‘‘raw’’ feature dataset.

suffered from overfitting in the training phase. Compared to the GRU, the LSTM tends to have more parameters (3 gates instead
of 2 gates) given the same level of model complexity. This can give the LSTM an edge in capturing more complex patterns in the
data but it can also make the LSTM more prone to overfitting. Lowering the model complexity and performing hyperparameters
tuning can definitely solve this problem. As for the CNN models, recent literature has suggested that they can outperform RNNs
in sequential modeling (Ghoshal and Roberts, 2020; Bai et al., 2018). However, in this case, the state-of-the-art CNNs could not
outperform other architectures. It is likely due to the fact that the long lookback period (24 h) of the time series seems to favor the
ability to retain long-range information about the RNNs over the ability to identify local patterns of the CNNs. The logistic model’s
simplicity results in lower precision than most models, but it outperforms XGBoost. The XGBoost, despite its similar nature with
the LightGBM, actually performed way worse than all other models in precision, with 2% precision lower than the 2nd worst one.
However, its financial metrics such as the Sharpe ratio are similar to those of the LightGBM models.
5. Conclusion and future work
5.1. Conclusion
Our analysis has pointed out that it is not inconceivable that the Bitcoin price history might contain some predictive information.
That information can even be enhanced through the use of several popular technical indicators so that they can be exploited by
quantitative methods to create a profitable trading strategy. Our detailed results show that our prediction models using the machine
learning approach trained on the Binance cryptocurrency exchange can create profits that are better than the classic Buy-and-Hold
strategy and provide much better precision than random guesses.
Our study has shown how several popular machine learning methods such as neural networks, decision trees, and logistic
regression performed on Bitcoin price trend prediction. The results turned out that the neural networks did the best compared
to other models, especially in the case of fewer features. However, among several neural network architectures, the state-of-the-
art CNNs did not outperform other architectures with comparable complexity as we expected (Ghoshal and Roberts, 2020). The
recurrent neural network, on the other hand, shows superior performance with the GRU architectures and, to a lesser extent, the
LSTM architectures. Between the two, the GRU performed better than LSTM. However, due to the limited sample size, we cannot
arbitrarily conclude that the GRU is better than the LSTM.
5.2. Future work
In the future, there are many things that are worth trying to improve the performance of our system:
1. Hyperparameters tuning:
In our study, we mostly used a predefined set of hyperparameters and architectures suggested by other studies for our machine
learning models. However, these hyperparameters are definitely not the optimal ones. Due to our limitation in time and
training system specification, we could not do this step but fine-tuning a machine learning system, especially in the case of
neural network models, can significantly improve its performance.
2. More feature engineering and feature selection :
Only a few technical indicators are chosen as the features in this study. In practice, there are a plethora of other technical
indicators that are yet to be tested. Aside from technical indicators, there can be other sources of data such as fundamental
data, and alternative data like news, Twitter, etc. The inclusion of new features and their selection can definitely improve
the performance of our system.

3. Ensembling models:
In this study, we only take into account the performance of every single model. However, the method of the ensemble can
also be applied to other machine learning models like neural networks. Robert, S. et al. (2020), in their work, mentioned an
improvement in their system using models ensembling.
4. Incorporation of transaction cost:
The omission of transaction costs in our performance evaluation is perhaps the most apparent flaw of our study. Transaction
costs can be difficult to model as they depend on the bid–ask spreads, the slippage, the volume of the executed transaction,
and various other aspects. A more thorough incorporation of the transaction cost could have been more beneficial for the
validation of the results.
5. Testing on other markets:
Despite that our predicting systems may work with the Bitcoin market, it does not mean they will work with other financial
assets. More tests should be made in other markets to verify the robustness of the models.
